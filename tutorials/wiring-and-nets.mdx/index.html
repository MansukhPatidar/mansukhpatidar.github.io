Wiring & Nets

Wires are how you create electrical connections between components. This tutorial covers drawing wires, understanding how breadboard strips work, and using nets to stay organized.

What you’ll learn

  • How to draw a wire between two holes
  • How breadboard strips connect holes automatically
  • Viewing and managing nets
  • Renaming and deleting wires
  • How power rails work

1. Draw a wire

  1. Switch to the Wire tool by pressing W or clicking the wire icon in the toolbar.
  2. Click on the first hole — it highlights to show it’s selected.
  3. Click on the second hole — a wire appears connecting the two.

That’s it. Two clicks, one wire.

💡 Tip: Press Esc to cancel a wire you’ve started but don’t want to finish.

2. How breadboard strips work

A real breadboard has internal metal strips that connect groups of holes. NoWires.AI replicates this exactly:

  • Terminal strips (rows A–E and F–J) — each group of 5 holes in a column is electrically connected. For example, holes A1 through E1 are all one strip.
  • Center channel — the gap between rows E and F separates the top and bottom halves. A1–E1 is one strip; F1–J1 is a separate strip.
  • Power rails — the long horizontal rows along the top and bottom edges, marked with + (red) and − (black/blue).

This means you don’t need to wire every connection manually. If you plug one leg of a resistor into A5 and one leg of an LED into C5, they’re already connected through the strip.

3. See a net highlighted

A net is a group of holes and wires that are all electrically connected.

To see a net:

  • Hover your cursor over any wire on the board.
  • All holes and wires in that same net highlight together.

This makes it easy to trace connections through your circuit without following individual wires.

4. The Nets drawer

Click the Nets icon in the sidebar to open the Nets drawer. Here you’ll see:

  • A list of all named nets in your project
  • Which holes and components are connected to each net
  • Color indicators for easy identification

The Nets drawer gives you a bird’s-eye view of your circuit’s connectivity.

5. Rename a net

By default, nets get automatic names. To give them meaningful labels:

  1. Open the Nets drawer.
  2. Click on a net name.
  3. Type a descriptive name like “VCC”, “GND”, “LED_ANODE”, or “SENSOR_OUT”.
  4. Press Enter to confirm.

Good net names make complex circuits much easier to debug and share.

💡 Tip: Name your power and ground nets first. It makes everything else easier to follow.

6. Delete a wire

Two ways to remove a wire:

  • Erase tool — press E, then click the wire
  • Select and delete — press V, click the wire, then press Delete

Deleting a wire removes only that wire. If other wires or strips still connect the same holes, the net remains intact.

7. Understanding power rails

The breadboard has four power rails:

RailPositionTypical use
Top + (red)Top edgePositive voltage (e.g., 5V)
Top − (black)Top edgeGround or negative
Bottom + (red)Bottom edgePositive voltage
Bottom − (black)Bottom edgeGround or negative

How these rails are configured depends on your project’s power settings. You’ll learn to customize this in Power Configuration.

To use a power rail, simply wire from a component pin to a hole on the rail strip.

  • How terminal strips auto-connect groups of 5 holes
  • Hovering to highlight entire nets
  • Using the Nets drawer to view and rename connections
  • Deleting wires without breaking other connections
  • The four power rails and their typical roles
