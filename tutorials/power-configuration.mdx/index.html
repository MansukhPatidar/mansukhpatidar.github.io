Set up your board's power rails — configure voltage, current limits, and bottom rail modes for different circuit requirements.

Power Configuration

Every circuit needs power. NoWires.AI lets you configure exactly how voltage is delivered through your breadboard’s power rails — just like choosing a bench power supply in a real lab.

What you’ll learn

  • How to access power settings
  • Configuring the top rail voltage and current
  • The four bottom rail modes and when to use each
  • How power rails map to the board visually
  • Practical examples for common circuit types

1. Access power settings

  1. Open your Project Properties (click the project name in the toolbar, or use the Project Organizer).
  2. Find the Power section.

Here you’ll see controls for the top rail and bottom rail configurations.

2. Top rail: voltage and current

The top power rail is your primary power source. You can configure:

  • Voltage — set the supply voltage (e.g., 3.3V, 5V, 9V, 12V)
  • Max current — set the current limit in milliamps

These values determine what appears on the red (+) rail along the top of the board.

💡 Tip: Match these settings to your actual power supply when using a physical NoWires board. Common values are 3.3V for ESP32 projects and 5V for Arduino projects.

3. Bottom rail modes

The bottom rail can operate in four different modes, depending on your circuit’s needs:

Same as top (both positive)

Both the top and bottom rails carry the same positive voltage. Use this when you need easy power access on both sides of the board without jumping wires across.

When to use: Simple circuits where you want convenient power on both edges.

Common ground (GND)

The bottom rail becomes ground (0V). This is the most common configuration.

When to use: Most digital circuits, Arduino projects, single-supply analog circuits. Your components connect to the top rail for power and the bottom rail for ground.

Negative rail (dual supply)

The bottom rail carries a negative voltage (e.g., −5V). Combined with the positive top rail, this gives you a dual-supply configuration.

When to use: Op-amp circuits, audio amplifiers, or any analog design that needs both positive and negative voltage rails.

Disconnected (floating)

The bottom rail has no electrical connection — it’s completely inactive.

When to use: When you only need one power rail, or when you want to use the bottom rail holes for general-purpose connections without any power implications.

4. How power rails map to the board

Once configured, the board visually reflects your power settings:

  • Top + rail (red) — carries your configured positive voltage
  • Top − rail (black) — typically ground, but depends on your configuration
  • Bottom + rail (red) — mirrors or differs from the top, based on your chosen mode
  • Bottom − rail (black) — follows the bottom rail mode setting

The rail labels on the board update to show the actual voltages, so you always know what voltage is on each rail.

5. Practical examples

Arduino Uno project (5V single supply)

  • Top rail: 5V, 500mA
  • Bottom rail mode: Common ground
  • Result: +5V on top, GND on bottom — clean and simple

ESP32 project (3.3V single supply)

  • Top rail: 3.3V, 300mA
  • Bottom rail mode: Common ground
  • Result: +3.3V on top, GND on bottom

Op-amp audio circuit (dual ±9V supply)

  • Top rail: 9V, 200mA
  • Bottom rail mode: Negative rail (−9V)
  • Result: +9V on top, −9V on bottom, with ground wired to the center channel

LED testing (single rail only)

  • Top rail: 5V, 100mA
  • Bottom rail mode: Disconnected
  • Result: Only the top rail is active — bottom holes are free for general wiring
